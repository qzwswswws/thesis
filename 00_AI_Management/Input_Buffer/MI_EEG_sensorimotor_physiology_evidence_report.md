## Paragraph 1

- **Core claim**

  Hand motor imagery is expressed mainly through central sensorimotor rhythm modulation over the hand area, with the strongest direct evidence centered on lateral central regions and possible added contribution from the midline. \[Pfu06, Mcf04\]

- **Mandatory papers**

  - \[Pfu06\]
  - \[Mcf04\]

- **Optional or provisional papers**

  - \[Pfu97\]

- **Exact quantitative findings**

  - \[Pfu06\]: 9 healthy right-handed subjects, 60 electrodes, hand MI produced broad ERD in the 10 Hz and 20 Hz ranges at C3 and C4 with contralateral dominance; the paper states that the most important sites for discrimination were C3, Cz, and C4; the most reactive mu components varied from 9 to 14 Hz, with mean reactive frequency 11.7 ± 0.4 Hz, mean ERD frequency 10.9 ± 0.9 Hz, and synchronized components at 12.0 ± 1.0 Hz. Verified pages: PDF pp. 1, 3 to 5. Verified items: Fig. 1, Fig. 2, Table 1, Table 2. \[Pfu06\]
  - \[Mcf04\]: 33 adults, 64 channels; mu analyzed at 8 to 12 Hz and beta at 18 to 25 Hz; imagery and movement both showed desynchronization; mu topographies showed bilateral sensorimotor foci, while beta showed a more midline central or frontocentral focus; channel effect F = 18.54, p \< 0.0001 and channel by frequency interaction F = 3.04, p \< 0.0001. Verified pages: PDF pp. 1, 4 to 8. Verified items: Fig. 2, Fig. 5, Table 1. \[Mcf04\]
  - \[Pfu97\] provisional: 3 subjects, best discriminative bands varied from 9 to 14 Hz and 18 to 26 Hz, with about 80% online accuracy. Full-text page trace not yet verified. \[Pfu97\]

- **Safest thesis wording**

  - “Healthy hand MI is associated with central mu and beta desynchronization, with the most informative activity centered on the lateral hand areas and, in some analyses, extending to the midline.” \[Pfu06, Mcf04\]
  - “The direct evidence supports prioritizing central sensorimotor sites, but not reducing the phenomenon to a single electrode pair.” \[Pfu06, Mcf04\]

- **Overclaims to avoid**

  - “C3 and C4 are always sufficient.”
  - “Cz is always equally important.”
  - “All subjects show the same central topography.” \[Pfu06, Mcf04\]

## Paragraph 2

- **Core claim**

  The physiology should not be written as one generic SMR effect, because mu and beta differ in spatial pattern, and user-level lateralization strength varies across groups. \[Mcf04, Zap20b\]

- **Mandatory papers**

  - \[Mcf04\]
  - \[Zap20b\]

- **Optional or provisional papers**

  - \[Neu99\]

- **Exact quantitative findings**

  - \[Mcf04\]: mu 8 to 12 Hz and beta 18 to 25 Hz showed distinct topographies; for imagery vs rest, the paper describes bilateral mu foci and a more centralized beta focus; Table 1 indicates mu maxima at lateral central or postcentral sites and beta maxima at midline-central or frontocentral sites. Verified pages: PDF pp. 4 to 8. Verified items: Fig. 2, Table 1. \[Mcf04\]
  - \[Zap20b\]: 20 right-handers and 20 left-handers; right parietal cluster showed stronger left-hand desynchronization than right-hand desynchronization in right-handers; during left-hand imagery, right-handers had stronger alpha desynchronization than left-handers, −2.59 dB vs −1.46 dB, p = 0.031; online accuracy was 51.54% ± 16.43 for right-handers vs 43.25% ± 8.24 for left-handers, Welch t(28) = 2.02, p = 0.027, d = 0.64. Accessible source is full HTML, not paginated; verified items: Fig. 3 and Fig. 4 in HTML. \[Zap20b\]
  - \[Zap20b\] also explicitly states that the clearest group difference was in alpha 8 to 13 Hz, not beta. \[Zap20b\]
  - \[Neu99\] provisional: abstract reports 4 trained subjects, 85% to 95% classification accuracy, contralateral ERD in all subjects, ipsilateral ERS in 2 subjects, and increased hemispheric asymmetry during feedback. Full-text page and figure trace not yet verified. \[Neu99\]

- **Safest thesis wording**

  - “Mu and beta rhythms should be discussed separately because they do not share the same imagery-related topography.” \[Mcf04\]
  - “Lateralization strength is not uniform across users, and handedness can alter the expression of alpha-mu desynchronization during hand imagery.” \[Zap20b\]

- **Overclaims to avoid**

  - “Mu and beta are fully independent.”
  - “Left-handers are poor MI users.”
  - “Handedness explains BCI performance by itself.” \[Mcf04, Zap20b\]

## Paragraph 3

- **Core claim**

  The temporal expression of MI rhythms is participant-dependent, and baseline timing can materially alter both apparent ERD and MI-vs-rest performance. \[Xu19, Rim23b\]

- **Mandatory papers**

  - \[Xu19\]
  - \[Rim23b\]

- **Optional or provisional papers**

  - \[Pfu05\]

- **Exact quantitative findings**

  - \[Xu19\]: 13 recruited healthy right-handed participants, 1 excluded; fixed mu band 8 to 13 Hz; window sizes 150 to 500 ms were tested; 150 ms was selected; generalized latency was 635 ms after cue onset; MI success rate improved from 58.47% with generalized latency to 74.14% with individualized latency; the comparison reported t(11) = 4.766, p = 0.001. Verified pages: PDF pp. 1, 4 to 9. Verified items: Fig. 4, Fig. 5, Fig. 7. \[Xu19\]
  - \[Xu19\] also reports that MI average success with 150 ms windows across all latencies was 58.02 ± 7.23%, and the 150 ms choice in MI was a preferred setting rather than a strongly significant MI-specific optimum. \[Xu19\]
  - \[Rim23b\]: 71 healthy right-handed MI-naive participants; baseline windows \[-5,-2\] s, \[-4,-1\] s, \[-3,0\] s, and \[-2,+1\] s; closer-to-previous-trial baselines produced stronger and more global ERD, p \< 0.01; Baseline 1 improved classification over Baseline 3 by +4% and over Baseline 4 by +8%; Baseline 2 improved over Baseline 4 by +7%; subject-specific baseline improved classification only +2%, not significantly. Verified pages: PDF pp. 1 to 4. Verified items: Fig. 1, Fig. 2, Fig. 3. \[Rim23b\]
  - \[Pfu05\] provisional: 9 able-bodied subjects; beta rebound at 23 to 29 Hz; 7 of 9 showed clear vertex rebound after feet imagery, but only 2 of 9 showed clear vertex rebound after hand imagery, though 5 of 9 showed significant contralateral hand-related rebound. Full-text page trace not yet verified. \[Pfu05\]

- **Safest thesis wording**

  - “The latency of detectable mu suppression varies across participants, and individualized timing can substantially improve single-trial MI detection.” \[Xu19\]
  - “Baseline timing is not a neutral preprocessing detail, because it changes both the apparent magnitude of ERD and MI-vs-rest separability.” \[Rim23b\]

- **Overclaims to avoid**

  - “150 ms is the universal optimal MI window.”
  - “635 ms is the correct onset for all subjects.”
  - “ERD differences across studies are purely physiological.”
  - “Hand MI has a robust universal beta rebound marker.” \[Xu19, Rim23b, Pfu05\]

## Paragraph 4

- **Core claim**

  Decoder design should be presented as constrained by physiology first, with engineering papers used only as secondary feasibility support. \[Pfu06, Mcf04, Xu19, Rim23b\]

- **Mandatory papers**

  - \[Pfu06\]
  - \[Mcf04\]
  - \[Xu19\]
  - \[Rim23b\]

- **Optional or provisional papers**

  - \[Zap20b\] for subject-variability support
  - \[Bla22\] as design-support only

- **Exact quantitative findings**

  - Central-channel relevance from \[Pfu06\]: most important sites were C3, Cz, C4. \[Pfu06\]
  - Band separation from \[Mcf04\]: mu 8 to 12 Hz and beta 18 to 25 Hz had distinct topographies. \[Mcf04\]
  - Timing variability from \[Xu19\]: individualized latency improved MI detection from 58.47% to 74.14%. \[Xu19\]
  - Baseline sensitivity from \[Rim23b\]: baseline placement changed performance by +4% to +8% depending on which baseline was compared. \[Rim23b\]
  - Design-support only from \[Bla22\]: best benchmark setup used 8 to 15 Hz, 15 to 22 Hz, and 22 to 29 Hz filter banks with a 1.5 s post-trigger window and about 74% accuracy. Full-text trace not yet completed. \[Bla22\]

- **Safest thesis wording**

  - “The physiology supports three decoder constraints: emphasis on local central channels, separate treatment of mu and beta features, and timing choices that respect inter-subject variability and baseline contamination.” \[Pfu06, Mcf04, Xu19, Rim23b\]
  - “Benchmark engineering studies may be cited only to show that these physiology-led choices are feasible in practice.” \[Bla22\]

- **Overclaims to avoid**

  - “Engineering benchmark results establish neurophysiological mechanism.”
  - “Physiology alone proves the final decoder architecture.”
  - “One benchmark window or filter bank is biologically optimal.” \[Bla22\]

## Claims that must not be written before PDF verification

- Any strong statement that feedback reliably increases hemispheric asymmetry in general, because \[Neu99\] is not yet fully traced. \[Neu99\]
- Any claim that \[Pfu97\] provides a stable quantitative benchmark for left-right MI beyond its very small sample and early setup. \[Pfu97\]
- Any claim that post-imagery beta rebound is a robust hand-MI biomarker, because \[Pfu05\] shows a stronger and clearer effect for feet than for hands. \[Pfu05\]
- Any claim that specific benchmark filter banks or a 1.5 s window are mechanistically grounded by physiology, because \[Bla22\] is engineering-support only and not fully traced. \[Bla22\]
