Abstract:

The accurate decoding of ElectroEncephaloGraphy (EEG) signals would bring us closer to understanding brain functionality, opening new pathways to fixing brain impairments and devising new Brain-Computer Interface (BCI)-related applications. The impressive success of deep convolutional neural networks extracting information from raw data in computer vision and natural language processing has motivated their investigation into EEG signal decoding. Consequently, a number of deep convolutional neural network models with state-of-the-art performance have been proposed in the literature for EEG signal decoding. However, because all these works aimed to find the model architecture with the best decoding accuracy, the model's size was left unbounded in that exploration. Considering the model size in the design of deep convolutional neural networks for EEG decoding could make their implementation on low-power microcontrollers (that may be integrated into a wearable system) with limited memory feasible. Thus, in this paper, we search for the most accurate deep convolutional neural network on the BCI Competition IV 2a dataset that can also fit in a microcontroller with less than 256KB SRAM. Specifically, we use a Neural Architecture Search (NAS) algorithm that considers, apart from the model's accuracy, the model size, the latency, and the peak memory utilization when running the model's inference. We compare our models with the model with the best decoding accuracy in the literature on the BCI Competition IV 2a dataset (baseline). We show that the discovered models could achieve similar accuracy to the baseline model while shrinking the memory footprint during inference by a factor of ≈ ×20, with a speedup in latency of up to ×1.7 on average.

SECTION I.

Introduction

The extraction of useful information from recordings of EEG signals using Machine Learning (ML) techniques has shown encouraging results in diagnosing neurological disorders such as epilepsy [1] and its treatment feasibility [2], diagnosing sleep disorders [3], and assisting stroke rehabilitation through Brain-Computer Interface (BCI) [4], [5]. However, even though ML-assisted EEG analysis has already made substantial progress in “tracking down” diseases and disabilities from impaired brain function, there is still much room for developing compact Deep Learning (DL) models with high accuracy that can also be implemented into small-memory, battery-operated Microcontrollers (MCUs).

Convolution Neural Networks (CNN)s are a type of Neural Network that perform convolutions between the previous layer's output or the input layer signal (commonly 2D signals) with different learnable kernels. CNNs have proven incredible effectiveness in feature extraction from raw data, as shown by recent advancements in Natural Language Processing (NLP) and Computer Vision (CV). Consequently, CNNs have been used to extract useful information directly from raw EEG signals in [6]. However, studies on efficient implementations of these CNN models on low-power MCUs have been left in shadow.

The hands-on design of a Deep Neural Network (DNN) for a given dataset is tedious or sometimes infeasible. Thus, Neural Architecture Search (NAS) algorithms [7]–​[13] have been studied to automate the search of the DNN Architecture that achieves the best accuracy on a given dataset. However, all previous NAS algorithms do not consider any constraint but only try to maximize the model's accuracy. Thus, the resulting DNN models are usually more suitable to run on powerful platforms such as GPUs or smartphones. However, the incessant requirement of running inference of DNNs on low-power with small memory capacity MCUs has shifted the focus of research to TinyAutoML [14], [15], i.e., NAS algorithms are trying to find the DNN Architecture that simultaneously achieves the best accuracy but can also be implemented on low-power MCUs with small memory capacity. By doing so, the AI technology for EEG-based applications can reach many more patients more conveniently and can be adjusted to every person individually (personalized healthcare), e.g., using wearables. In this study, we consider μNAS [16], a TinyAutoML framework to explore different DNNs, for the first time to the best of our knowledge, suitable to be implemented on MCUs with small memory capacity on a dataset of EEG recordings [17].

The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Section II describes the state-of-the-art CNN model for EEG decoding on the BCI Competition IV 2a dataset, which we consider the baseline model. Section III presents an overview of the TinyAutoML algorithm μNAS that we used in this work. Section IV demonstrates the feasibility of MCU-sized deep CNNs with similar accuracy to the baseline model, while conclusions are made in Section V.

SECTION II.

EEG Decoding Testcase: The Shallow ConvNet Architecture

The authors of [6] proposed several custom CNN architectures, ranging from 2 to 31-layer CNN networks, for decoding the EEG signals in the BCI IV 2a dataset. All these deep CNN models could reach similar or even better decoding accuracies than Filter Bank Common Spatial Patterns (FBCSP) [18], a method known for winning several EEG-decoding competitions. The Shallow ConvNet model, among all the proposed architectures, exhibited the best average accuracy on different subjects.

Fig. 1.

The shallow ConvNet architecture.

A. EEG Decoding Dataset

The BCI IV 2a dataset consists of 22-electrodes EEG recordings that capture the brain activity of nine subjects associated with motor imagery. Each subject i has its own set of EEG recordings captured during the execution of four types of body movements. The j-th entry of the i-th subject in the dataset is represented as Di={Xj,Yj}, j from 1 to the total number of recorded trials, where Xj∈RE×T and Yj∈RT, with E denoting the number of recorded electrodes, T the number of timesteps after the discretization of each trial, and Yj being the label with one of the four types of body movements, namely “Left Hand”, “Right Hand”, “Feet” and “Rest” in each timestep of the j-th trial.

B. The Shallow ConvNet Architecture

Shallow ConvNet matches the temporal and spatial convolution in the first two layers with the spatial filters (bandpass and Common Spatial Patterns (CSP)) in FBCSP. The rest of the pipeline consists of a squaring nonlinearity layer, a mean pooling layer, and a dense layer with the softmax activation function. These layers match the trial logarithmic variance calculation in FBCSP. The architecture of Shallow ConvNet is shown in Figure 1.

SECTION III.

μNAS Overview

μNAS represents NN architectures as computational graphs. Graph edges depict the sequence of layers in the NN architecture, and graph vertices correspond to operators (acting on these layers), such as the convolution and max/mean pooling operators. Furthermore, μNAS adopts the TensorFlow Lite inference runtime. Therefore, each layer must allocate memory buffers for its inputs and output before executing the NN inference. These buffers are allocated in MCU's SRAM memory and are only deallocated once all computations of an operator that involve the data in these buffers are completed. The data required to perform all computations of an operator comprises the so-called working set. Thus, the memory occupied by the working set, which corresponds to the Peak Memory Usage (PMU), must not overflow SRAM's memory capacity. Finally, all NN parameters are stored in MCU's Flash memory, which must have the appropriate size to store them.

A. Search Space

Table I The NN design parameters defining the μNAS search space.

Typically, Neural Architecture Search (NAS) algorithms restraint the options of NN architectural parameter layer-wise (e.g., number of tensor/channels) while also limiting the layer connectivity choices for the following two reasons:

  • Considering large models, more layer connectivity options result in a broad search space that may lead to a poor or infeasible solution.

  • For larger models, evaluating convolution layers with minute differences in channel size would not result in a discernible accuracy improvement.

These drawbacks do not affect the MCU-sized search space's tractability since the models we are searching for are significantly smaller. However, using a high granular search space would make a significant difference in smaller networks that are extremely memory sensitive to layer and connectivity changes. Even a minor change in the value of the corresponding architectural parameter plays a crucial role in determining whether the generated model can fit in a memory-constrained MCU or not. For that reason, μNAS has been designed to navigate through a giant sea of minor architectural differences essential for discovering high-accuracy with low-memory footprint designs. All possible architectural choices taken under consideration during the neural architecture search are listed in Table I.

B. Operator Reordering

Since the computational graphs in μNAS may have branches, multiple topological graph orderings (different execution paths) exist. Different execution paths mean different PMU for the same NN architecture. In contrast to NN models with only one execution path, the inference engine has to choose which operator is better to execute next, determining which tensors must be preserved in the working set, ultimately affecting PMU. Thus, μNAS tries to minimize the PMU by using a dynamic programming algorithm [19] where all possible execution paths are first enumerated, and that one with the lowest PMU is selected (see Fig. 2).

Fig. 2.

Operators reordering example annotated with the impact on peak memory usage (PMU) and the tensors, which must be present in the working set.

C. Search Algorithm & Objective

μNAS tries to find the smallest NN model that exhibits the best accuracy, and its inference requires the lowest PMU and has the shortest latency. However, since all the previous objectives are orthogonal to each other, there is no single optimal solution, e.g., the NN model's inference cannot require the least PMU without sacrificing some accuracy and vice-versa. Thus, μNAS aims to find NN models as closely as possible to the Pareto curve of all these objectives. In order to approach the Pareto curve, μNAS employs an Aging Evolution [20] optimization algorithm with an objective function, the weighted combination of the individual objectives [21]

For a diverse evaluation of each objective, the λt weights may change in every optimization step. Initially, a population of P random architectures is picked. Then, after sampling S candidate architectures from the Population in every optimization step, the architecture with the highest objective function value, i.e., the parent architecture, is chosen. Then, a new Child architecture is created by applying random permutations to the Parent architecture. Finally, the new Child architecture is added to the Population and the History while the oldest architecture in the Population is discarded from it. These steps are repeated until the History size reaches C candidate architectures from which the most accurate one is selected (see Algorithm 1).

SECTION IV.

Evaluation

In this section, we show that by employing μNAS, a TinyAutoML algorithm, we could find NN models that present SOA decoding accuracy on the BCI IV 2a competition dataset while requiring less memory during inference. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work to leverage a TinyAutoML algorithm, to discover MCU-sized NNs for decoding EEG data.

We compare our results to those of the Shallow ConvNet, which presented the best accuracy in decoding the EEG signals of the BCI IV 2a dataset for both low and high frequencies 1 (see Tables II & III). It is worth mentioning that model size is not a problem in the case of Shallow ConvNet as we need only around 150KB of MCU's Flash memory, which is relatively much less than most Flash memories (1MB of Flash is pretty standard). Therefore, we do not include any model size comparisons but focus only on the PMU, although μNAS managed to find architectures with model sizes of less than 150KB.

A. μNAS Configurations

The main obstacle in implementing deep CNN models on MCUs is their limited memory. Because the model size of ConvNet fits in memory of typical MCUs, as we mentioned earlier, while its PMU is prohibitive for MCUs SRAM size, we focus on μNAS configurations that restrain the PMU of candidate tiny-models to typical MCUs SRAM sizes, while of course searching for the best model accuracy. Thus, we considered three μNAS configurations that constrain the PMU of the candidate models to 64KB, 128KB, and 256KB, respectively. Additionally, apart from splitting the dataset into training and test sets, μNAS offers the option to split further the training set into a new training set and a validation set to tune the architectural parameter of the candidate models, eschewing the bias on the training set. Thus, we also consider three different configurations of μNAS for train/validation set slits, namely 90/10, 80/20, and 70/30.

B. μNAS Models vs Shallow ConvNet Model

In Table IV, we present the highest accuracy models we obtained with μNAS on the testset per subject for the high and low frequencies, setting as PMU bound in the optimization process the highest value that permits μNAS to achieve accuracy similar to the Shallow ConvNet. The candidate model architectures during the exploration were trained with 130 epochs for the high and 70 epochs for the low frequencies, respectively (the same training configuration as the Shallow ConvNet). Table IV also shows the μNAS wining models' latency in Multiply-Accumulate (MACs) operations, as proved in [16] that MACs are a suitable approximation for measuring latency in MCUs environments. In general, the discovered models achieved almost identical decoding accuracies to the Shallow ConvNet model while diminishing PMU by a factor of ≈ ×20 (see Table V). Simultaneously the resulting models need significantly fewer MACs operations, thus effectively reducing the models' latency.

SECTION V.

Conclusions

In this paper, we sought the most accurate deep convolutional neural network on the BCI Competition IV 2a dataset that fits on a microcontroller with less than 256KB of SRAM using a TinyAutoML approach. More specifically, our CNN architectural search considers, in addition to the model's accuracy, the model size, the latency, and the PMU when performing the model's inference. We compared our discovered models with the Shallow ConvNet model, which shows the highest decoding accuracy on the BCI Competition IV 2a dataset (our baseline model). Finally, we demonstrated that the obtained models from the TinyAutoML approach we followed can attain the same accuracy as the baseline model while reducing the PMU during the inference time by a factor of ×20 on average and latency by up to ×1.7. Our study concludes that TinyAutoML technology can adjust the Machine Learning models for EEG-based applications to any patient individually (personalized healthcare) and make feasible the execution of these models on wearable devices.
