Abstract:

Although Morse telegraphy via shortwave commu-nication has fallen out of widespread use, it maintains its unique value in specific fields such as military communications, emergency services, and navigation. We have designed a PyTorch-based neural network architecture of Convolutional Recurrent Neural Network(CRNN) and Bidirectional Gated Recurrent Unit (BiGRU), and the trained model enables fast decoding of Morse signals. In this paper, the pt-format model of PyTorch is converted to an RKNN model, which is then deployed to Rockchip's industrial-grade general-purpose System on Chip(SoC) RK3568. The real-time decoding efficiency of Morse signals is significantly improved through the implementation of a sliding window mechanism and NPU hardware acceleration. Empirical data from our experimentation reveal that on the RK3568 platform, the decoding time for a one-minute-long audio signal is significantly reduced from 12.63 s using CPU decoding to 1.67 s with NPU decoding, which indicates an approximate seven times to eight times improvement in decoding efficiency.

SECTION I.

Introduction

Since the new century, communication technology has developed at an unprecedented rate, with Morse communication gradually receding from public view. As the Morse code is still employed in certain specific fields, such as the military, emergency services, navigation, and assistive speech [1] [2], the decoding technology of Morse audio signals is still in evo-lution. In the traditional Morse signal communication system, the process of sending and receiving information relies on the manual operation of telegraph operators. The practice not only places high demands on the proficiency of telegraph operators, but also has a highly detrimental effect on the precision of their listening and transcription capabilities under the conditions of low signal-to-noise ratios(SNR), and fast transmission rates [3]. Additionally, the market and practical applications are increasingly demanding greater portability in decoding devices. Therefore, we have developed a neural-network-based Morse communication system capable of automatically undertaking the task of transmitting and receiving Morse signals. The system has been effectively deployed into mobile terminals to meet the demands of real-world application scenarios.

The recognition of Morse signals has been studied by schol-ars over recent decades. Traditional methods typically adopt processing strategies based on audio time-domain information, which often require filtering and binarization in order to facilitate effective recognition based on the level-amplitude variation of the Morse signal [4] [5]. Some papers have used support vector machines (SVM) [6], hybrid expert models [7], etc. on this basis to recognize Morse signals and improve their performance. The bottleneck of these methods is that they greatly rely on the preprocessing of audio signals and show high sensitivity to signal interference, which is obsolete today. In recent years, research based on time-frequency diagrams has led to a rapid development of Morse signal recognition technologies. With the introduction of machine learning, Qu et al. utilize dynamic thresholding and the K-means algorithm to achieve the automatic Morse recognition [8], but the algorithm demonstrates limited robustness to noise. Conventional machine learning methods for Morse decoding require both Morse detection [9] [10] and Morse recognition, and errors are accumulated during the process. Wang et al. use Hidden Markov Models and Deep Neural Networks (HMM-DNN) to introduce deep learning (DL) techniques into Morse signal recognition for the first time [11]. The deep learning method is an end-to-end decoding technology that employs straightforward steps and avoids the error accumulation problem. However, their exploration of the low SNR ratio scenario was not comprehensive. Moreover, all of the above methods are based on computer platforms for research, and the situation on lightweight terminals is not explored.

The structure of the paper is as follows: Section 2 discusses the technical background and the neural network employed; Section 3 explores hardware acceleration on mobile platforms; Section 4 details the experimental results and analysis; and Section 5 summarizes the conclusions and future research directions.

SECTION II.

Technical Background and Neural Network

A. Morse Signal Transformation

The structure of Morse signals consists of five fundamental time intervals: dots (short signals), strokes (long signals), dot intervals (gaps between short signals or long signals), char-acter intervals (gaps between characters), and word intervals (gaps between words) [11]. In this paper, the selection of these intervals adheres to the traditional Morse coding ratio rule of 1:3:1:3:7. In terms of Morse signal processing, the representation of Morse signals on time-frequency diagrams is more explicit than on time-domain diagrams. Time-frequency diagrams can show the time and frequency characteristics of the Morse signals more efficiently, thus presenting the Morse information clearly. In contrast, time-domain diagrams are susceptible to noise and other interference. In Fig. 1, both the time-frequency diagram and the time-domain diagram are effective in reflecting the Morse information when the SNR is 5 dB. However, in Fig. 2, when the SNR is −5 dB, the dot-drawing information of the Morse signal in the time-frequency diagram can still be clearly observed, whereas it is challenging to identify the effective information in the time-domain diagram. The result highlights the superiority of time-frequency diagrams for Morse signal recognition in low SNR environments. Accordingly, we employ the transformation of the input Morse audio signal into the time-frequency diagram as the input of the neural network.

B. Neural Network

In this paper, a CRNN model based on Convolutional Neural Network(CNN) and Recurrent Neural Network(RNN) is applied [12]. Specifically, the BiGRU is selected as the RNN component of the network. The model outperforms in decoding Morse audio signals. The CRNN model first uses convolutional layers for feature extraction, where the input time-frequency map is subjected to two convolutional layers with the channel dimension expanded to 128 and the frequency dimension contracted to 1. The feature extraction is then performed using a three-layer BiGRU. Next, sequence feature prediction is performed using a three-layer BiGRU, and final sequence decoding is performed using a transcription layer to decode the Morse audio efficiently. The structure of the model is shown in Fig 3.

Fig. 3.

Neural network structure.

For a proficient telegraph operator, when SNR is equal to or less than −6 dB, there is a high probability of Morse signal reception errors. The Convolutional Recurrent Neural Network (CRNN) model demonstrates a notable enhancement in decoding accuracy, achieving levels exceeding 99.4%, under conditions characterized by Gaussian white noise and a signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) maintained above −8 dB. This improve-ment is observed notwithstanding the relatively prolonged duration required for the decoding process.

Furthermore, when utilizing the PyTorch framework-generated pt-format model for decoding on Linux-embedded systems, it was observed that the decoding rate is suboptimal, making it challenging to meet the real-time demands. Conse-quently, the RK3568 processor was chosen as the decoding hardware platform in order to accelerate the decoding process and facilitate real-time performance.

SECTION III.

Hardware Acceleration for Mobile Terminals

A. Transformation of Morse Decoding Models

In this paper, we employ the Rockchip RK3568 as the experimental platform, which is a general-purpose SoC designed for the mid-to-high-end market. The RK3568 chip is based on a 22-nanometre process technology and integrates a quad-core A55 processor with ARM architecture and a Mali G52 2EE graphics processor, which provides the chip with excellent computational power and graphics performance [13]. The RK3568 chip has a built-in standalone neural network processor (NPU), which makes it suitable for executing lightweight AI applications and provides powerful hardware support for real-time decoding of Morse signals.

The acceleration of the RK3568's NPU is mainly based on the RKNNLite development tool provided by Rockchip officially to invoke the NPU to accelerate Morse decoding. However, the NPU acceleration via RKNNLite requires the employment of an RKNN model architecture. In Fig. 4, the pt-format model is first converted to an ONNX model by calling the conversion function in the ONNX library, and then the ONNX model is converted to the RKNN model by the conversion tool provided by Rknn-toolkit2.

Fig. 4.

Model conversion path.

However, a new issue is introduced. The RKNN model requires a fixed shape of the input data, which contradicts the large fluctuating characteristics of the speech file's time length. The most intuitive way to solve this issue is to cut the spectrogram from the speech file conversion into fixed-length slices to be fed into the model for decoding before splicing. In fact, this method has a potential drawback. At the cut point, a complete Morse code may split into two pieces, which leads to incorrect decoding, resulting in a decrease in decoding accuracy.

B. Sliding Window Mechanism

To address the above issue, we adopt a fixed-length data slicing method called the sliding window decoding mechanism to achieve high-accuracy decoding with a fixed input data shape. The method fixes the input data shape length of the RKNN model to 3000 points, corresponding to a speech time length of one minute.

The core idea of the sliding window decoding mechanism is as follows: first, a segment of a predefined length is extracted starting from the beginning of the Morse data, which is then fed into the model for decoding. After the decoding of the segment, the decoding result is temporarily stored. Next, in order to obtain a new data segment, the sliding window is moved along the data stream so that the start of the window is 400 points before the end of the previous slice, i.e., equivalent to 8 seconds of speech. The same decoding operation is performed on the newly intercepted data fragment to obtain a new decoded output. To construct the final decoded sequence, a splicing technique is employed. Specifically, the last 200 points (corresponding to 4 seconds of decoded information) of the previous decoded result are removed, and the first 4 seconds of information are removed from the latest decoded result. The remaining two parts of the decoded information are spliced to form an updated and extended segment of the decoding result. This process is repeated until the entire data stream has been processed. Through the iterative mechanism, it is possible to achieve continuous decoding of the entire data stream while maintaining a fixed window size for model input and continuity of output.

The operation diagram is shown in Fig. 5.

Fig. 5.

Sliding window mechanism.

In Fig. 5, spec is the spectrogram resulting from the transformation of the original file, spec_trunc[] is a sliding window with a fixed length of 3000 points, and m_last[] is employed to store the final decoding result.

  • Firstly spec_trunc[] captures the initial data slice, denoted as A. A is then fed into the network for decoding, and the resulting decoded result is stored in m_last[].

  • spec_trunc[] slides to the position of 8 s before the tail of slice A, i.e., point L, as the starting point to intercept a fixed -length data slice, denoted as B. The same decoding operation is performed on B to obtain a new decoding result. It is necessary to note that a complete Morse code may be split into two parts at the cut point, resulting in an error in decoding at the cut point. Therefore, it is necessary to discard the last four seconds of the decoding result of slice A and the initial four seconds of the decoding result of B. In Fig. 5, it is essential to eliminate the decoding information from point M to point N in m_last[] and the decoding information from point L to point M of the decoding result of B. Then, the remaining parts are spliced together to obtain the new decoding result m_last[].

  • Follow this mechanism until processing to the last inter-cepted data slice denoted as D. If the length of D is less than 3000 points, zero-padding is applied at its tail to 3000 points before decoding. Repeat the above splicing operation to get the complete file decoding result.

SECTION IV.

Experimental Results and Analysis

In order to validate the effectiveness of the method proposed in this paper, this section designs experiments based on For Linx's OK3568-C development platform with a computer platform (CPU AMD Ryzen 7 5800H with Radeon Graphics, GPU NVIDIA GeForce RTX 3060 Laptop GPU) to validate the decoding efficiency of the pt-format model and the RKNN model.

A. RK3568 Platform

On the RK3568 platform, the pt-format model relies only on the RK3568's built-in CPU for decoding, while the RKNN model uses the RK3568's NPU for decoding. In order to validate the efficiency of the RKNN model, a set of experiments is designed to document the average decoding time of both models for different lengths of Morse audio signals. In Fig. 6, when decoding a one-minute audio signal, the pt-format model exhibits an average decoding time of 12.63 s, compared with a reduction of 1.67 s for the RKNN model. The result reflects that the RKNN model achieves approximately a 7.5 times improvement in decoding efficiency. Furthermore, the advantage of the RKNN model becomes more apparent as the length of the test audio signal increases. For a seven-minute audio signal, the average decoding times of the pt-format model and the RKNN model are 81.5 s and 6.94 s respectively. Compared with the pt-format model, the RKNN model provides a decoding efficiency improvement of more than 11 times.

Fig. 6.

Average decoding time under different audio lengths for the pt-format model and RKNN model.

B. Computer Platform

In the context of the laptop platform, the pt-format model makes use of the GPU for the decoding operation. This subsection shows the performance of the pt-format model utilizing GPU decoding versus that of the RKNN model utilizing NPU decoding. As shown in Fig. 7, for a one-minute-long Morse audio, the pt-format model slightly outperforms the RKNN model in terms of decoding efficiency. However, as the length of the audio signal increases, the RKNN model decoding time is gradually better than the GPU decoding of the pt-format model. When the audio signal duration is two minutes, the decoding efficiency of the two models is basically equal. When the duration of the audio signal is three minutes, the decoding time of the pt-format model is 3.71s, while the decoding time of the RKNN model is only 3.46s, which indicates that the decoding efficiency of the RKNN model has already exceeded that of the GPU decoding of the pt-format model. In addition, as the duration of the audio signal increases further, the difference in decoding efficiency between the two models becomes more significant.

Fig. 7.

Average decoding time under different audio lengths for the pt-format model utilizing GPU decoding and RKNN model utilizing NPU decoding.

Although the pt-format model shows superior speed in short-duration audio signal decoding processing, the RKNN model presents a more significant advantage in decoding efficiency as the duration of the audio signal increases.

The RKNN model effectively reduces the demand for computational resources and accelerates the overall processing speed, and at the same time, it facilitates the shift from traditional decoding platforms to lightweight devices for Morse de-coding. This also demonstrates the excellent application value of the RKNN model in driving efficient decoding operations on mobile or resource-constrained devices.

SECTION V.

Conclusion and Future Work

A. Conclusion

In this paper, we present an innovative real-time decoding method for Morse code based on domestic SoC. The method mainly introduces the RKNN model and incorporates a sliding window mechanism to address the specific problems that may be encountered when employing the model. We significantly reduce the time required for Morse code decoding on lightweight terminals. The decoding time for a one-minute-long Morse audio signal is significantly reduced from 12.63 s by the pt-format model to 1.67 s by the RKNN model, achieving an efficiency improvement of about 7 to 8 times. In addition, the method demonstrates comparable or even superior decoding performance to conventional decoding terminals. In conclusion, our work successfully achieves real-time decoding of Morse signals on a lightweight terminal, which marks a significant advance in the field of efficient communication signal processing on resource-constrained devices.

B. Future Work

Although the sliding window mechanism can effectively solve the problem of incorrect decoding of the Morse code at the cut point, it operates with redundancy and repeated decoding of specific audio segments before discarding them. This process inevitably increases the computational overhead and leads to longer decoding time. Future work can focus on optimizing the mechanism to further reduce the unnecessary computational process and thus improve the overall decoding efficiency.
